A gentle holding routine for a suspected meniscus or ACL injury. Low-impact, equipment-free, and limited to the safe staples that calm a knee and keep the surrounding muscles working — nothing that loads or twists an unstable joint. Read first — the pain rule

Stay at or below 3–4 / 10 discomfort. Mild ache is fine; sharp pain is a stop sign.

Move slowly and within a comfortable range. If a movement hurts, shrink the range or skip it. Stop the session and speak to a clinician before resuming if you notice any of these:

  • ✕ Sharp or stabbing knee pain
  • ✕ The knee locking or catching
  • ✕ A sense of the knee giving way
  • ✕ New or increasing swelling

The week

Your 7-day plan

Same core routine most days, with the emphasis rotating and two deliberately lighter days so the knee gets recovery. Never push an emphasis into pain — it just means an extra set or a little more attention on those movements. Tap a day to mark it done.

The routine

The 11 movements

This is the full daily session. On lighter days do only the warm-up, the isometrics (quad sets, heel slides) and the cool-down stretches.

This is general guidance, not a diagnosis or a prescription. The movements and doses are conservative starting points used to settle a knee before assessment — not tailored to your specific injury, which only an examination (and likely a scan) can confirm. A torn meniscus and a torn ACL want slightly different things, so treat this as a bridge. Once your surgeon assesses you, the real rehab plan will look different; let their guidance override this.

If pain, swelling, or instability is worsening rather than holding steady before end of July, get assessed sooner rather than waiting.
